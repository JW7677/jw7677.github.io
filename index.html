Anthropic Sues the Pentagon — Day 10 of the Dispute

1. Anthropic Sues the Pentagon Over "Supply Chain Risk" Label

Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits today against the Department of Defense, calling the government's "supply chain risk" designation an "unlawful campaign of retaliation." The suits were filed in U.S. District Court for Northern California and the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals. Anthropic argues the label — typically reserved for firms tied to foreign adversaries — is being used to punish the company for refusing to remove AI safeguards.

Sources: CNBC Axios NBC News

2. At the Heart of the Dispute: Surveillance and Autonomous Weapons

The ban stems from Anthropic's refusal to allow Claude to be used for mass domestic surveillance of U.S. citizens or for autonomous weapons systems. The Pentagon insists it must be able to use AI tools for "all lawful purposes" without restrictions from a private company, especially in national security emergencies. Negotiations broke down after months of back-and-forth, leading to the February 27 federal order halting business with Anthropic.

Sources: CNN NPR

3. Defense Tech Companies Drop Claude Following Blacklist

In the wake of the Pentagon's blacklist, defense technology companies began instructing employees to stop using Claude and switch to other AI models. President Trump gave federal agencies six months to phase out Anthropic's technology entirely. The designation affects not just government agencies but also any company doing business with the Department of Defense.

Sources: CNBC CBS News

4. The Backlash Backfires: Claude Hits #1 on the App Store

The Pentagon's ban had an unexpected effect: it made Claude enormously popular with the public. Within 24 hours of the blacklist announcement, Claude climbed to the #1 spot on the U.S. Apple App Store, displacing ChatGPT. Over 1.5 million users reportedly joined the "QuitGPT" movement in solidarity with Anthropic.

Sources: ALM Corp

5. Legal Experts: Pentagon's Designation May Not Hold Up in Court

Legal analysts at Lawfare suggest the Pentagon's "supply chain risk" designation against Anthropic is on shaky legal ground, as the label has historically been used only for companies tied to foreign adversaries like China or Russia. Experts argue the designation as applied here is unprecedented and likely to face serious judicial scrutiny.
